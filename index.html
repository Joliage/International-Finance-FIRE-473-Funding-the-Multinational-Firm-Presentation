14.1The advisor & the reality check

Why the investment bank goes first

What the bank does for you

  • Stays in touch with potential foreign investors and their current requirements.
  • Helps navigate institutional barriers and decides if, when, and where to cross-list.
  • Prepares the prospectus, helps price the issue, and maintains an aftermarket to support the price.
The reality check

A firm known only at home is usually not visible enough to attract global investors. Jumping straight to a global equity issue tends to fail.

The proven sequence is gradual penetration: a credible eurobond first, then listings, then equity — earning name recognition at each step.— the lesson from Novo (Chapter 13)

14.2The settled debate

Traditionalists meet Modigliani & Miller — in compromise

When taxes and bankruptcy costs are considered, a firm has an optimal financial structure — the mix of debt and equity that minimizes the cost of capital for a given level of business risk.

Why debt lowers cost — at first

  • Debt is cheaper than equity, and interest is tax-deductible: its after-tax cost is kd(1 − t).
  • Adding low-cost debt pulls the weighted average (WACC) down.

Why it eventually reverses

  • More debt raises perceived financial risk, so the cost of equity ke climbs.
  • Past a point, fear of insolvency spikes the cost of both debt and equity — WACC turns back up.
If a new project's business risk differs from existing projects, the optimal debt/equity mix shifts to balance business vs. financial risk.

14.2The U-shaped curve · Exhibit 14.2

The cost of capital is U-shaped in leverage

  • The debt ratio = total debt ÷ total assets, at market values.
  • As it rises, WACC falls (cheap, tax-favored debt), then rises (insolvency risk) — a U-shape.
  • The bottom is a broad, flat range — roughly 30%–50% debt — where cost barely changes.
  • Where a firm sits within that range depends on its industry, its sales/income volatility, and the collateral value of its assets.
kWACC = ke·(E/V) + kd(1 − t)·(D/V)

14.2Optimal structure & the multinational

Four variables that bend the domestic theory for an MNE

1 · Availability of capital

Global market access lets an MNE keep its marginal cost of capital constant over a wide budget range — small domestic firms can't.

2 · Diversification of cash flows

Internationally diversified cash flows are less variable, which can, in theory, support higher debt ratios.

3 · Foreign exchange risk

Foreign-currency debt adds FX gains/losses to its true cost — sometimes a lot. tool next →

4 · Investor expectations

Global investors apply U.S./UK-style norms. Debt ratios up to ~60% are accepted; higher is a hard sell.

14.2Availability & diversification

A constant marginal cost — and a diversification caveat

The availability advantage

  • With global access, large new fundraises don't push up the MNE's marginal cost of capital.
  • MNEs in illiquid home markets that haven't globalized look like small domestic firms — leaning on internal funds and bank loans, and sometimes over-borrowing.
The diversification debate

Because country returns aren't perfectly correlated, an MNE's diversified cash flows should be steadier — supporting more debt.

But the evidence pushes back: U.S. MNEs actually carry lower debt ratios than domestic peers — facing higher agency costs of debt, political risk, FX risk, and asymmetric information.

14.2FX risk & the cost of debt

The 5% loan that really cost 9.375%

A U.S. firm borrows CHF 1,500,000 for one year at 5%. Over the year the franc appreciates from CHF1.5000 to CHF1.4400 per dollar. The true dollar cost combines the interest rate and the currency move:

kd$ = [(1 + kdCHF)(1 + s)] − 1
= [(1.0500)(1.041667)] − 1 = 9.375%
Dollars received$1,000,000
Repay (CHF 1,575,000 ÷ 1.44)$1,093,750
Currency change  s+4.167%
Effective cost9.375%

The extra 4.167% is recorded as an FX transaction loss — and it's tax-deductible. A depreciating foreign currency would have made the debt cheaper than 5%.

Global norms override national ones

14.3The core distinction · Exhibit 14.3

Issuance raises cash. Listing builds visibility.

Equity issuance → cash

  • IPO — first sale of a private company's shares to the public.
  • SPO / follow-on (FO) — selling additional shares later.
  • Euroequity — selling in two or more countries at once.
  • Directed issue — sale aimed at one specific market or investor.

Equity listing → visibility

  • Shares are traded on an exchange in a specific country market.
  • Cross-listing puts shares on an exchange abroad to widen the investor universe.
  • Depositary receipts (DR) — a bank certificate standing in for foreign shares.
  • A listing often precedes an issuance, preparing the market.
An issuance needn't be public: a firm can sell to private investors via a private placement — but the holder gives up an open, liquid market for the shares.

14.3Inside the IPO

Going public: the price of the spotlight

  • An underwriting syndicate of investment banks prepares filings and a prospectus.
  • The initial sale is usually 15%–25% of the firm (recently as little as 6%–8%).
  • The shares traded publicly are the public float (or free float).
  • Going public means continuous disclosure — costly, and it hands information to competitors, customers, and suppliers.
15–25%
typical share of ownership sold in an IPO
Shares only raise capital at issuance. Daily price swings change owners' returns — not the company's capital.

14.3Euroequity & privatizations

Euroequity: one IPO, many countries, at once

  • An IPO on multiple exchanges in multiple countries simultaneously, underwritten by an international syndicate.
  • "Euro" is just a label — not European issuers, not euro-denominated.
  • Shares are sold in tranches — allocations to underwriters for their geographic markets — to raise funds and ensure worldwide liquidity.
  • The biggest were privatizations of state-owned enterprises, often profitable quasi-monopolies with strong credit.

Chapter 14 · Funding the Multinational Firm
14.4The vocabulary & the origin

ADRs, GDRs, and a clever 1927 workaround

  • A depositary receipt (DR) is a negotiable certificate from a bank representing foreign shares held in trust.
  • ADR = traded in the U.S., in U.S. dollars. GDR = traded outside the U.S.
  • Each DR can equal a multiple or fraction of a home share, so the U.S. price lands in a sensible range.
  • Dividends flow foreign-firm → custodian → issuing bank, which converts to dollars for holders.
Born to beat a rule

The first ADR (1927, by J.P. Morgan) was for British retailer Selfridges. Britain barred its firms from registering shares abroad without a British transfer agent.

DRs sidestep this by creating a synthetic share abroad — no actual registration of shares outside the home country.

14.4How issuance works · Exhibit 14.5

Buying a Brazilian company through an ADR — in 7 steps

  1. U.S. investor instructs a broker to buy the Brazilian shares.
  2. U.S. broker places the order with a local broker in Brazil.
  3. Brazilian broker buys the shares, delivers them to a custodian bank.
  4. U.S. broker converts dollars to reais to pay for the shares.
  5. Custodian notifies the U.S. depositary bank of the deposit.
  6. Depositary bank issues and delivers the ADRs to the U.S. broker.
  7. U.S. broker delivers the ADRs to the investor.

Once they exist

  • Intra-market trading (ADR to ADR) is ~95% of all DR trading.
  • Settles in dollars on T+3 via the DTC.
  • Arbitrage keeps home and U.S. prices in line.

14.4Program structures · Exhibit 14.6

Sponsored or not — and three levels of commitment

Sponsored vs. unsponsored

  • Sponsored: the foreign firm requests it and pays the costs.
  • Unsponsored: a U.S. securities firm starts it; rare, still needs SEC approval.

The three ADR levels

Level I  OTC / pink sheetsEasiest · home-country disclosure · ~6 wks · no new capital
Level II  U.S. exchange listingFull SEC + U.S. GAAP · ~13 wks · no new capital
Level III  new equity issueFull SEC + prospectus · ~14 wks · raises capital
Higher level = more disclosure and cost, but Level III is the only one that lets a foreign firm raise new equity in U.S. markets.

14.4Global registered shares

GRS vs. ADR: one share everywhere, or a strategically priced receipt

  • A GRS is the identical share traded across exchanges without conversion — one share = one share, just in different currencies.
  • A German share at €4.00 with a $1.20 rate is $4.80 as a GRS — too cheap for the NYSE.
  • As an ADR, bundle 4 shares → $19.20, inside the $10–$20 "strategic pricing" zone that maximizes U.S. liquidity.
  • GRSs keep all voting rights and ease comparison; ADRs win on target pricing in markets like the U.S.
€4.00 × $1.20 = $4.80  (GRS)
$4.80 × 4 = $19.20  (ADR)

14.5SEC Rule 144A & QIBs

Skipping SEC registration — legally

  • A private placement sells a security to a small set of qualified institutional buyers — insurers, pension & investment funds.
  • Rule 144A (1990) lets QIBs trade privately placed securities without SEC registration and without holding-period limits.
  • A QIB invests $100M+ on a discretionary basis. The PORTAL system supports a secondary market.
  • Since registration is the big barrier for foreign firms, 144A is attractive abroad.
First mover · Atlas Copco

The Swedish engineering firm was the first foreign firm to use Rule 144A — raising $49M via an ADR placement inside a larger $214M euroequity issue (1990).

14.5Private equity funds

Buy, fix, and resell — or fund a startup

Private equity funds

  • Limited partnerships of institutions & wealthy investors (e.g., endowments).
  • Buy control of firms, take them private, improve management, resell in 5–7 years.
  • Often use heavy debt; charge 2% of assets + 20% of profits.
  • A lifeline for mature emerging-market family firms too small/invisible to globalize alone.

vs. venture capital

  • VC operates mainly in highly developed markets.
  • Invests in startups, aiming to exit via an IPO.
  • Little VC exists in emerging markets — hard to exit via IPO in an illiquid market.
  • PE invests in already-profitable firms and has a longer horizon.

14.5Why cross-list at all?

Four reasons to list shares abroad

  • Improve share liquidity and support a secondary market for new issues.
  • Raise the share price by escaping mis-pricing in a segmented, illiquid home market.
  • Increase visibility with customers, suppliers, creditors, and host governments.
  • Create a liquid market for shares used to acquire firms and pay local staff.
Case · Kosmos Energy

After its 2011 U.S. IPO NYSE: KOS, Kosmos listed on the Ghanaian Stock Exchange — Ghana being where it made its discoveries and earned nearly all its income.

14.6Why eurobonds exist

Four forces behind the eurobond market

Light regulation

Bonds in a foreign currency fall outside any single nation's regulatory domain.

Less disclosure

Cheaper, faster to market than an SEC-registered issue.

Favorable tax

Interest often free of withholding tax; issued in bearer form — tied to tax anonymity.

Ratings & reputation

Issuers can withhold a poor rating, so name recognition drives terms — big MNEs & sovereigns pay least.

Foreign bonds carry national nicknames: Yankee (U.S.), Samurai (Japan), Bulldog (U.K.) — a foreign issuer borrowing in that country's currency and market.

14.6Global Finance in Practice 14.3

Islamic finance: profit-sharing instead of interest

  • Built on Sharia principles from the Qur'an — restricting how businesses fund themselves.
  • Core rules: no earning interest, profit and loss shared, no speculation, invest only in halal activities.
  • An Islamic bank can't pay depositors interest — depositors are effectively shareholders whose returns track the bank's profits.
A Sharia-compliant mortgage

The bank buys the home, then resells it to the buyer at a higher price payable over years. The markup is implicit interest by Western thinking — yet it conforms to Sharia.

In the U.S. and U.K., that markup is not tax-deductible the way interest is.
Muslims are roughly one-fourth of the world's population; predominantly Islamic countries produce about 10% of global GDP.

14.7Sources of funds · Exhibits 14.8–14.9

Funding from inside — and outside — the MNE

Internal (within the MNE)

  • From the parent: equity (cash or real goods), cash loans, leads & lags on payables.
  • From sister subsidiaries: loans and timing of intrafirm payables.
  • Subsidiary borrowing with a parent guarantee.
  • Generated by the subsidiary: depreciation and retained earnings.

External (outside the MNE)

  • Debt from the parent's country: banks, money & security markets.
  • Debt from outside: local-currency, third-country, eurocurrency.
  • Local or global equity: local shareholders, JV partners.
MNEs usually minimize equity in a subsidiary to reduce capital at risk — debt is the preferred form, once the subsidiary can access it.

14.7Whose norms? Whose interests?

A tug-of-war over the subsidiary's balance sheet

  • Financial-structure norms vary widely across countries, less within one — driven by tax, governance, bond-market depth, and more.
  • For the subsidiary's debt ratio, count only debt from outside the MNE: a parent loan ≈ equity to host countries (subordinated, no insolvency threat).
FX concern: a startup prefers to service debt in local currency.   Tax concern: MNEs maximize subsidiary debt for the interest deduction — so host governments cap deductible debt.
Host government wants
  • All equity funding
  • Profits reinvested, not repatriated
The MNE wants
  • All debt funding
  • Debt service as a contractual way to remit cash & minimize equity at risk

14.7Three stages · Exhibit 14.10

Financing needs change across the life cycle

Stage 1

Startup

  • Funded almost entirely by the parent
  • Mostly equity; debt kept low to ease cash-flow pressure
  • Just needs to pay its bills
Stage 2

Growth

  • Capital needs rise with sales
  • Local debt becomes available as cash flow grows
  • Host limit: debt ≤ ⅓ of total capital
Stage 3

Maturing

  • Growth slows; financing needs level off
  • Retained earnings build equity; profits repatriated
  • Debt in many maturities & currencies

CASESeptember 2008 & the downgrade

The markets freeze, and the rating breaks

  • Sept 2008: Lehman fails, AIG is seized — banks stop refinancing debt for everyone.
  • CEMEX Q3 sales: Spain −26%, Mexico −10%, U.S. −25%+; U.S. housing starts ≈ ¼ of two years prior.
  • A $711M FX-hedge loss is unwound; $5.5B of debt comes due in 2009.
  • The covenant (Net-Debt/EBITDA < 2.5) is breached; the August waiver lapses.
Downgrade

End of 2008: Moody's and Fitch cut CEMEX to speculative grade (Ba1 / BB+).

Falling from investment to speculative grade shrinks capital availability and raises borrowing costs at the worst possible moment.

CASEThe 2009 problem · Exhibit E

Repeat 2008 and repay $5.5B — and the cash runs out

If 2008's cash flows simply repeat in 2009 and CEMEX repays the full $5.5B on schedule, it ends the year at a negative $2,944M balance — not financially feasible.

The escape: restructure the levers and refinance most of the $5.5B — pushing maturities into later years while keeping creditors satisfied.

Levers CEMEX can pull

  • Cut or convert the dividend ($476M)
  • Sell assets (Australia ~$400–500M, Hungary/Austria, Canary Islands)
  • Slash capex to maintenance only (~$700–800M)
  • Issue new equity (~$400–500M rights)
  • Refinance the bulk of the $5.5B

What the banks need — and what you'll be asked

Creditors want three things

  • Continue to earn a market (risk-adjusted) rate.
  • A realistic plan for full repayment.

REVIEWKey terms · from the glossary

Tap any term to reveal its plain-English meaning

Eurobond
A bond sold outside the country whose currency it's in — e.g., a dollar bond sold outside the U.S.
Foreign bond
A foreign issuer's bond sold inside a national market, in that country's currency — Yankee, Samurai, Bulldog.
ADR
A U.S. bank certificate that stands in for foreign shares, traded in dollars on U.S. exchanges.
GDR
Like an ADR, but the certificate trades outside the U.S.
GRS
One identical share that trades across exchanges without conversion, priced in each market's currency.
QIB
A "qualified institutional buyer" — an institution investing $100M+ that can trade private securities.
Rule 144A
A 1990 SEC rule letting QIBs trade privately placed securities without SEC registration.
Private equity
Investors who buy control of firms, take them private, improve them, and resell within a few years.
Euroequity
An IPO sold on several countries' exchanges at the same time.
Tranche
A slice of an issue allocated to underwriters for sale in their geographic market.
Free float
The share of stock actually held by the public and freely traded.
Net debt
Interest-bearing debt minus cash — what's owed beyond the cash on hand.
EBITDA
Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation & amortization — a proxy for core cash earnings.
Investment grade
A credit rating signaling reliable repayment — BBB- / Baa3 or higher.
Speculative grade
A rating below investment grade (BB+ / Ba1 or lower), implying higher default risk.
Credit spread
The extra interest over a benchmark a borrower pays to compensate for its credit risk.

CH. 14Summary

The chapter in eight ideas

  • Globalizing capital is a staged journey — debt first, then equity listings, then issuance.
  • An optimal structure minimizes WACC; for an MNE it's bent by access, diversification, FX risk, and investor norms.
  • An MNE's marginal cost of capital can stay flat across a wide budget — a domestic firm's can't.
  • Foreign-currency debt's true cost = interest ± the currency move.
  • Equity paths: euroequity, directed issues, depositary receipts, private placement.
  • DRs let segmented-market firms improve liquidity and raise new shares abroad.
  • Debt markets offer many maturities, structures, and currencies — bank loans, euronotes, eurobonds.
  • Subsidiary structure follows host-country norms and shifts across the life cycle — and overreaching on debt can be fatal (CEMEX).
